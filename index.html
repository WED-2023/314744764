Q&A:

Differences between web 1.0, web 2.0, and web 3.0

Who generates the content in websites in Web 1.0, Web 2.0, and Web 3.0?

Answer:
Web 1.0 is static and read-only, the content on the website managed by the organization or website owner.
Web 2.0 is dynamic and user-generated, users can edit and share content and there are social platforms for content sharing and collaboration.
Web 3.0 is decentralized and powered by blockchain technology.
There are many internet connected devices that provide data and there are bots that generate content.
Many websites are automated and use AI to generate content.
Token incentives encourage users to contribute content

Use Case: Creating a website for a family owned business
 Web 1.0: The family owns and maintains by themselves a static website where users can only read information about their store.
Web 2.0: The family opens a facebook page to advertise their business, where users can comment and share their posts.
On the page they link to sell their products online through Etsy.
Web 3.0: The family uses an AI to manage their website and look up the latest trends in their industry to update their website.
They use blockchain to manage their inventory and sales.


How is user data handled differently in Web 1.0, Web 2.0, and Web 3.0?

Answer:
 Web 1.0 has no user data interaction.
Web 2.0 centralizes user data in platforms, it is collected and monetized by the platform.
Users have little control over their data and privacy they are subject to the platform's terms and conditions.
Companies can sell user data to third parties, personalize ads and content in ways that may not be transparent to the user.
Web 3.0 gives users control over their data using blockchain.
Users can choose to monetize their data themselves and have more control over their privacy.
They may choose to share their data with companies in exchange for tokens or other incentives.
Otherwise if they choose to keep their data private they can do so using encryption and other privacy tools.

Use Case: A company collecting user data to market better for their target audience
 Web 1.0: Employees scour the web to find blogs about their products and manually enter the data into company databases.
Web 2.0: The company pays social media platforms like instagram, where they can target ads to users based on their interests and demographics.
They also use cookies to track user behavior on their website and personalize the user experience.
Web 3.0: The company offers users tokens in exchange for sharing their data, which is stored on a blockchain.
Users can choose to share their data with the company and receive tokens in return, or keep their data private.


How do transactions differ in Web 1.0, Web 2.0, and Web 3.0?

Answer:
 Web 1.0 has no online transactions.
Web 2.0 uses centralized payment systems like credit cards and PayPal.
Many payments previously done in person are now done online.
Web 3.0 uses decentralized cryptocurrencies and smart contracts.

Use Case: purchasing a brand new computer
 Web 1.0: Customer browses through e-commerce catalogs to find the computer they want, then calls the store to place an order.
Web 2.0: Customer browses Amazon, places an online order and the payment is processed through Amazon's payment system.
Web 3.0: Customer visits a decentralized marketplace where payments are made using cryptocurrencies and governed by smart contracts.
